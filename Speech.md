# The Machine and the Mind: What AI Is Actually Doing to Our World

## Opening — The prediction that aged badly

*[pause.]*

In 2016, in a lecture hall in Toronto, one of the most important scientists of our age stood in front of a room full of specialists and told them, in plain terms, to stop training radiologists. Within about five years, he said, deep learning would read medical images better than any human being could. The radiologist's days were numbered. It was, he said, just completely obvious.

The man saying this was not a crank or a pundit. He was Geoffrey Hinton — the researcher more responsible than almost anyone alive for the technology we are here to talk about. His work on neural networks, and the work of his students, lit the fuse of the entire modern AI revolution. He is so respected that he would later win the Nobel Prize. And he is so deeply alarmed by what he helped create that in 2023 he resigned from Google specifically so he could speak freely about the dangers of artificial intelligence. When this man makes a prediction about AI, you would be wise to listen.

And he was wrong.

*[pause.]*

Nearly a decade later, radiologists have not been replaced. There is a *shortage* of them. Demand has gone up, not down. AI now helps read scans — it flags, it measures, it sorts — but it does so alongside the radiologist, not instead of her.

I open with that story because it is the most honest thing I can tell you about this subject. If the person who built the engine can be that confident, that specific, and that wrong about where it would go, then the rest of us should hold our predictions loosely. The technology is real. It is powerful. And its effects on our work and our world are genuinely, stubbornly hard to predict — even for the people who understand it best.

So the task is to hold two ideas at the same time, without collapsing into either one. This is the most consequential technology of our lifetimes — and almost every confident forecast about it, in both directions, deserves a raised eyebrow. Not blind enthusiasm. Not comfortable denial. Both eyes open.

Over the next forty-five minutes I want to walk through four things. First, *why this is happening now* — the history, quickly and without jargon. Second, *what is already happening* — the part we can actually observe, today, rather than guess at. Third, *what comes next* — where the smartest institutions in the world disagree, and they disagree dramatically. And finally, *a way of thinking* about all of it that survives contact with reality.

I've done my homework. Almost everything I'll tell you comes from named, serious sources — Goldman Sachs, McKinsey, the International Monetary Fund, the World Economic Forum, Stanford University, the US Federal Reserve, Pew, the World Bank — and a Nobel Prize-winning economist who thinks most of the others are badly mistaken. I'll tell you who said what. And I'll tell you where I think the truth is hiding.

Let's begin.

## Part One — Why now? Seventy years of winter, three years of spring

The first thing to understand is that artificial intelligence is not new. It is *old*. The term was coined in 1956, at a summer workshop at Dartmouth College, by a handful of mathematicians who confidently predicted that a machine with human-level intelligence was about twenty years away.

They were wrong too. Spectacularly wrong, for half a century. The history of AI from the 1950s to roughly 2012 is a history of what researchers themselves called "AI winters" — cycles of grand promises, thin results, collapsing funding, and disappointment. Every generation declared the breakthrough was imminent. Every generation was humbled.

So the real question is: why did the winter finally end? Why, after seventy years of letdown, did our phones suddenly start writing poetry and our colleagues start worrying about their jobs?

The honest answer has three parts, and none of them requires a technical background.

The first part is **data**. The internet happened. By the 2010s, humanity had digitized an almost unimaginable quantity of text, images, and recorded knowledge. For the first time, there was something for a learning machine to actually learn from — the accumulated written output of our species.

The second part is **computing power**. It turns out the specialized chips originally built to draw graphics for video games — called GPUs — are extraordinarily good at exactly the kind of mathematics AI requires. A company called Nvidia, which most people had barely heard of a decade ago, became one of the most valuable companies on Earth essentially because it makes the shovels for this gold rush. *[pause.]* The picks and the shovels. In every gold rush, it's the same story.

The third part — the genuine breakthrough — arrived in 2017. Eight researchers at Google published a paper with a slightly cheeky title: "Attention Is All You Need." It introduced an architecture they called the Transformer. I'll spare you the mathematics. What matters is this: the Transformer gave machines a far better way to handle language — to grasp that the meaning of a word depends on the other words around it, sometimes words very far away. The "T" in ChatGPT stands for Transformer. Every major AI system you've heard of — ChatGPT, Gemini, Claude — is built on that one paper.

Put those three forces together — oceans of data, mountains of cheap computation, and one elegant idea — and you get something researchers call *scaling laws*. This is the discovery that quietly drives the whole field: make these models bigger, feed them more data, give them more computing power, and they reliably get better. Not a little better. Predictably, dramatically better. And nobody has yet found the ceiling.

That is the entire story of "why now." The ideas were waiting. The fuel finally arrived. ChatGPT launched at the end of 2022, and the spring we are living in began.

Now — and this matters — *spring is not summer.* The fact that progress has been fast does not guarantee it continues at the same pace, and it does not mean the economic payoff has already arrived. Hold that thought. We'll come back to it.

## Part Two — What is already happening

Before we argue about the future, let's pin down the present — because the present is startling enough, and most of it is not in dispute.

### The technology itself

The most reliable, non-commercial source here is Stanford University's AI Index — an independent academic report that isn't trying to sell you anything. Three findings stand out.

First, **capability**. These systems now match or beat expert human performance on a range of hard exams. A systematic review found that GPT-4 scores between eighty and one hundred percent on the United States Medical Licensing Examination — the exam that stands between a medical student and a career. A specialized version scored over ninety-five percent. The test that takes a human the most stressful year of their life to prepare for, a machine now passes comfortably.

Second — and almost nobody appreciates this one — **cost**. Stanford found that the price of running one of these models for a given task fell more than two-hundred-and-eighty-fold in about two years. In plain terms: something that cost twenty dollars in late 2022 cost about seven cents two years later. Intelligence of a certain narrow kind is collapsing in price the way computing power and lighting and long-distance calls collapsed before it. And when something useful becomes nearly free, it does not stay in the laboratory. It floods into everything.

Third, **adoption**, and here is the number that still amazes me. ChatGPT reached one hundred million users in about two months — the fastest any consumer technology had ever spread, by a wide margin. Most tools take years to find that many people. This took a single winter. Behind that headline, Stanford found the share of organizations using generative AI in at least one business function more than doubled in a single year, from about a third to over seventy percent. The US Census Bureau, surveying real firms, gives a soberer figure — by late 2025, roughly eighteen percent of American companies reported actually using AI in their operations, though among large employers it's closer to a third. So: spreading fast, but still early. Hold on to that gap. It matters.

Now I want to dwell on one word, because it's the word that makes AI different from almost every other technology in living memory. The word is *universal*.

Most powerful technologies are specialists. The MRI machine transformed medicine and did nothing for accounting. The spreadsheet transformed accounting and did nothing for surgery. Each was revolutionary in its lane and irrelevant outside it.

AI is not like that. Economists keep a special category for the rare technologies that break out of every lane at once and rewire the whole economy. They call them *general-purpose technologies*. There are only a handful: the steam engine, electricity, the computer, the internet. Each one didn't just improve a single industry — it became a new foundation that *every* industry was eventually rebuilt upon. The consensus forming across the IMF, McKinsey, and Stanford is that AI belongs on that very short list.

Here's how universal it really is. The same underlying technology — and this is the remarkable part, it really is largely the *same* technology — is at this moment drafting legal contracts and reading radiology scans. Writing software and designing new drug molecules. Tutoring a child in mathematics and helping a screenwriter with dialogue. Answering customer calls, summarizing financial reports, translating between a hundred languages. *[pause.]* Those are not different tools. Under the hood, they are the same kind of system. And that is why there is no longer a safe lane to stand in. Whatever the field, this one is coming to the desk. The only choice is whether you meet it on your terms or on its own.

### Promise and peril, both already here

Let me make the present concrete with two true stories that, side by side, contain the whole paradox.

The first is about a mother and her young son. For three years the boy suffered chronic, mysterious pain. The family saw seventeen different doctors — seventeen — and no one could connect the symptoms into a diagnosis. Exhausted, the mother typed his entire medical history into ChatGPT. It suggested a condition called tethered cord syndrome. A specialist confirmed it. The boy had surgery. *[pause.]* Now, I tell that carefully — it's one reported case, not a study, and the AI did not "cure" anyone; a human surgeon did. But it shows you something real: a pattern-matching machine, with the whole medical literature in its memory, saw a thread that seventeen tired human experts had missed. That is augmentation, and it is happening today.

The second story is about a lawyer named Steven Schwartz. In 2023 he needed to write a brief, and he asked an AI chatbot to find supporting cases. It delivered — beautifully. Case names, citations, quotations, judicial reasoning, all articulate and confident. There was just one problem. The cases did not exist. The machine had invented them, not out of malice but because that is what it was built to do: produce text that *sounds* right. When the court couldn't find the cases, Schwartz did something very human — he went back and asked the AI whether they were real, and it said yes, so he believed it again. He was sanctioned five thousand dollars, the case was dismissed, and the American Bar Association now calls what followed "a growing national epidemic" of fabricated citations in court filings.

Hold those two stories together, because neither one alone is the truth. The same kind of system that caught what seventeen doctors missed will also invent a fact with total confidence and look you in the eye while doing it. Capable and unreliable, at the very same time. That is not a temporary bug we are about to fix. For now, it is the nature of the thing.

### The labor market is already shifting

Here is where "what's already happening" gets uncomfortable, and where I think the headlines are looking in the wrong place. The fear everyone voices is mass unemployment. That is not what the data shows yet. What the data shows is something more specific, and more poignant: **AI is breaking the bottom rung of the career ladder.**

In May 2025, Oxford Economics reported something that had not happened in roughly forty-five years: the unemployment rate for recent college graduates rose *above* the national rate. Read that again. The people who did everything they were told — went to school, earned the degree — are now having a *harder* time finding work than the general population. A Stanford study using actual payroll records found that for the youngest workers, those twenty-two to twenty-five, employment in the most AI-exposed jobs fell about thirteen percent relative to their older colleagues.

Why the young, specifically? Think about what a junior person actually does in almost any knowledge profession. The junior lawyer reviews the contracts and the documents. The junior accountant checks the records and the audit logs. The junior analyst builds the financial models and the slide decks. The first-year does the grunt work. And those tasks have always been two things at once: they're how the work gets done, *and* they're how a young person learns the craft and earns their stripes. The apprenticeship. *[pause.]* And the apprenticeship is exactly what today's AI does well.

One fact crystallizes it. Last year Bloomberg reported that OpenAI hired more than a hundred former investment bankers — not to do banking, but to *train its AI to build the very financial models that junior bankers build*. The entry-level job is being studied, distilled, and automated by the same people who used to do it.

That creates a problem we have barely begun to reckon with. The new human role, increasingly, is "checker" rather than "doer." The machine drafts; a person reviews. The machine builds the model; a person checks it. Which sounds fine — until you ask where the checker's judgment comes from. It comes from years of having been the doer. From making the mistakes. From grinding through ten thousand contracts so that the ten-thousand-and-first sets off an alarm in your gut. Automate away the apprenticeship, and we may raise a generation that never builds the judgment we'll later need them to have. We would be eating the seed corn.

It is even reshaping the architecture of organizations. For a century, firms were pyramids — a broad base of juniors narrowing to a few seniors at the top. The trend now, described by Gartner and by the consulting firms themselves, is toward a diamond: a thin entry level, a thick experienced middle, a narrow top. Fewer doors in. The ladder isn't just harder to climb. Some of the lower rungs are being quietly sawn off.

## Part Three — What comes next, where the experts disagree

Now we leave the observable present for the contested future. And here I have to be honest, because the easy move for a speaker is to grab the most dramatic number and run. I won't. I'm going to show you the actual range of expert opinion — because the *range itself* is the most important fact in this talk.

### The trillion-dollar argument

Start with the optimists. **Goldman Sachs**, in an influential report, estimated that generative AI could raise global GDP by seven percent — about seven trillion dollars a year — over a decade. Seven trillion: roughly the economies of Japan and Germany combined, conjured out of productivity. **McKinsey** concluded it could add between two-point-six and four-point-four trillion dollars a year to the world economy. And **PwC**, the most bullish, projected AI could make the global economy nearly sixteen trillion dollars larger by 2030 — a fourteen percent boost, more than the current output of China and India put together.

*[pause.]* Staggering. If you only read headlines, that's the story you absorbed: a multi-trillion-dollar tidal wave.

Now meet the man who thinks those numbers are fantasy. **Daron Acemoglu** is an economist at MIT who won the Nobel Prize in 2024. He is not a crank and not anti-technology. And in a paper with the deadpan title "The Simple Macroeconomics of AI," he reaches a wildly different conclusion: over the next decade, AI will add perhaps **one to one-and-a-half percent to GDP** — not per year, over the *entire ten years*.

*[land.]*

Look at what just happened. Goldman says seven percent. PwC says fourteen. A Nobel laureate says one. These are not quibbles at the margin; they're off from each other by a factor of seven, of ten, of fourteen. This is one of the largest disagreements among serious experts about any major economic question of our time.

How is that even possible? Because they're measuring different things. The consulting firms are measuring *potential* — the theoretical ceiling, what's possible if every business adopts AI smoothly and captures all the value on the table. Acemoglu is measuring *probable reality* — that only a fraction of tasks will be worth automating, that adoption is slow and messy, and that some automation is, in his phrase, "so-so": it replaces a worker without actually doing the job much better.

And here's the evidence for caution. In 2025, researchers at MIT looked at corporate AI projects and found that despite thirty to forty billion dollars of enterprise spending, roughly *ninety-five percent* of generative-AI pilots delivered no measurable financial return. Ninety-five percent. *[pause.]* That figure has been criticized as too neat — it's become more slogan than statistic — but the truth underneath it holds: there is an enormous gap between what AI can do in a demo and what it actually delivers on a balance sheet.

We've seen this pattern before. Economists call it the productivity paradox. In the 1980s, as computers flooded into offices, the economist Robert Solow quipped that you could see the computer age everywhere except in the productivity statistics. The gains did come — but they took about fifteen years, because using a new technology well means rebuilding how you work, not just buying the tool. We may be living inside exactly that lag right now.

So who's right — Goldman or the Nobel laureate? *[pause.]* The honest answer is we don't know yet, and anyone who claims certainty is selling something. The truth is probably somewhere in the wide space between them — and which end we land closer to depends less on the technology than on the choices we make over the next ten years.

### Jobs — the contested future

The headline figure you've heard is Goldman's: **three hundred million jobs** exposed to automation worldwide. Real number, real report, and it sounds like the apocalypse. But notice the word — *exposed*, not eliminated. For most of those jobs, AI automates a *portion* of the tasks, not the whole role. Goldman's own estimate is that even if this plays out over a decade, unemployment rises by under one percentage point. A real disruption. Not the end of work.

The **International Monetary Fund** gives the most careful global picture: about **forty percent of jobs worldwide are exposed** to AI. And here's the twist that should get our attention: in advanced economies, that figure climbs to around *sixty* percent. For most of history, automation came for physical labor — the factory floor, the assembly line — and the desk worker was safe. This wave is the first that climbs *up* the income ladder, not down. It is aimed at cognitive work: drafting, analyzing, summarizing, researching, coding.

But before anyone despairs, the hopeful data is just as well-sourced. The **World Economic Forum** projects that by 2030, AI and related trends will displace about ninety-two million jobs — and create about a hundred and seventy million, a net *gain* of nearly eighty million. And **PwC**, studying nearly a billion job postings, found that in the industries *most* exposed to AI, the number of jobs is still *growing*, and workers with AI skills command a wage premium of about fifty-six percent. So far, the evidence says, AI is making skilled people more valuable, not redundant.

### History rhymes — the women at the switchboard

*[pause.]*

I want to tell you about a job that no longer exists, because I think it's the most useful thing in this entire talk.

In 1950, one of the most common occupations for a young woman in America was telephone operator. To make a call, you didn't dial — you picked up, and a human being, almost always a woman, physically connected your line to another by hand at a switchboard. Around three hundred and fifty thousand women worked as operators for the phone company, and perhaps a million more ran switchboards in offices and hotels and factories. By one count, roughly one in thirteen working women in America sat at a switchboard. It was respectable, stable, middle-skill work. It was a path.

And then it was automated. Over decades, the telephone company replaced those operators with automatic switching — the dial, and later the buttons, that let you connect your own call. By mid-century, more than half the network had converted. A job that had employed a vast share of the nation's women simply dissolved.

Now here's why it matters, and here's the part that should give both pause and comfort. Two economists, Feigenbaum and Gross, studied exactly what happened to those women, city by city, as the switches were thrown. Their findings map almost perfectly onto our moment. First: the women *already working* as operators when automation arrived were hurt — a decade later, more likely to be in lower-paid work, or out of the workforce. The cost was real, and it fell on real people. Second: the entry pipeline slammed shut — in cities that automated, the number of young women hired as operators dropped by fifty to eighty percent. *[pause.]* Sound familiar? That is precisely the broken bottom rung we just talked about, playing out a century ago.

But third — and this is the comfort: the next generation of young women did *not* end up unemployed. They found work. The economy had generated new kinds of jobs — more clerical roles, more service roles, occupations that in some cases hadn't existed before — and they flowed into them. The job vanished. The workers, eventually, did not.

I find that history honest in both directions, and I'd resist hearing only the half you prefer. The pessimist's half is true: automation really does destroy specific jobs, really does hurt the people in them, and really does close the door on the next cohort. The optimist's half is also true: economies are astonishingly good at inventing new work, and people are astonishingly good at adapting to it. The open question — the one nobody can answer yet — is about timing and rungs. Does the new work arrive fast enough to catch the people the old work let go? And when it appears, will there be a ladder into it, or just a closed door? For the operators, the adjustment took a generation and a great deal of private pain. Making this transition faster and gentler than that one is not the machine's job. It's ours.

### The wider board — inequality, nations, and a $589 billion Monday

AI's reach doesn't stop at the office door. Three quick widenings of the lens.

First, **inequality**. The IMF's central warning is that AI is likely to widen gaps — between workers, and between countries. Those who wield it pull ahead; that's the wage premium. And the advanced economies, better wired and better capitalized, are positioned to capture most of the gains, while lower-income countries risk being left behind — the IMF estimates the growth boost to rich economies could be more than double that for poor ones. The technology that could lift the world might, left alone, deepen its divides instead. That's a policy choice, not a law of nature.

Second, **geopolitics**. AI has become an instrument of national power, because the things that produce it — advanced chips, vast data centers, and the electricity to run them — are now strategic assets, like oil or steel before them. That's why, since 2022, the United States has restricted exports of its most advanced AI chips to China; why the policy keeps lurching back and forth; why countries now talk about "sovereign AI." Let me make it concrete with the single most dramatic moment of the era. In January 2025, a relatively small Chinese startup called DeepSeek released a model nearly as good as the best American systems but, it claimed, built far more cheaply. The implication tore through the markets in hours: if frontier AI doesn't require tens of billions in hardware, what happens to the firms betting tens of billions on hardware? On a single Monday, Nvidia lost about five hundred and eighty-nine billion dollars of its value. In one day. *[pause.]* The largest one-day loss for any company in the history of the stock market. One model, from one startup, on the other side of the world, erased more wealth in an afternoon than most countries produce in a year. The lesson under the drama: the pricing power of the giants, and the whole investment thesis behind this boom, is more fragile than the hype suggests.

Third, **the physical footprint**. We imagine AI as something ethereal, living "in the cloud." It does not. It lives in enormous buildings full of hot chips that drink electricity. The International Energy Agency projects that the electricity used by data centers worldwide will more than double by 2030 — to roughly the entire current consumption of Japan. In the United States, data centers already draw over four percent of all electricity, and that could double again within a few years. *[pause.]* "Intelligence," it turns out, is becoming an industrial commodity measured in gigawatts. The mind runs on coal and gas and sun and uranium, just like everything else.

### Bubble or foundation?

Which brings us to the question hanging over all of it: is this a bubble?

The scale of investment is hard to fathom. The four largest technology companies spent over four hundred billion dollars on AI infrastructure in a single year — the biggest one-year corporate investment surge in history. McKinsey projects the global build-out could require nearly seven trillion dollars by 2030. And there are real warning signs. Analysts point to what they call "circular financing": the big cloud companies invest in AI startups; those startups spend the money right back on the cloud companies' services; the cloud companies then book booming AI revenue; and investors use that revenue to justify higher valuations and pour in still more. *[pause.]* Money chasing its own tail, each lap making the numbers look bigger. The most telling detail of all: the fiercest skeptic about whether any of this will pay off works *inside Goldman Sachs* — one of their own senior analysts has publicly doubted the returns. When a single firm is both the loudest optimist and home to one of the loudest skeptics, you're looking at a field that does not know its own future.

So — is it a bubble? Here's the most important thing I can offer, and I'd ask you to hold it carefully: "is it a bubble?" and "does it matter?" are two different questions, and the answer to both can be yes. History is full of technologies that were *both* a financial bubble *and* a genuine revolution. In the 1840s, Britain went mad for railways; a speculative mania inflated and burst, ruining many, and roughly a third of the lines that were approved were never even built. A bubble by any definition. *[pause.]* And yet the railways that *did* get built remade the modern economy. The same with the internet: in 2000 the dot-com bubble burst, the tech-heavy market fell nearly eighty percent, famous companies vanished overnight — and the internet still went on to reshape every corner of human life. So when someone asks whether AI is a bubble, my answer is: quite possibly, and that doesn't settle the question that matters. The speculative froth may well be destroyed — some of these valuations and companies will not survive — and the underlying technology may *still* transform the world. The bankrupt railway investor and the railway that changed history were part of the same story.

## Closing — A way of thinking, when no one knows

*[pause.]*

So where does that leave us? I've handed you a Nobel laureate who says one percent and a consulting firm that says fourteen. Three hundred million jobs exposed and a hundred and seventy million created. A machine that passes the medical boards and a machine that invents court cases out of thin air. The largest corporate investment in history, and the skeptic inside the bank who thinks it may not pay. A job that vanished in 1950 and the workers who, in time, found other work.

You could be forgiven for concluding that nobody knows anything. *[pause.]* But that's not quite right, and I want to leave you with something firmer than a shrug.

Here is what I believe the evidence genuinely supports. The technology is real, and it is not going away. The capability is genuine, the cost is collapsing, the adoption curve points up. Anyone waiting for this to turn out to be a fad is making a serious mistake; this is closer to the arrival of electricity or the internet — a general-purpose technology that will, over decades, work its way into nearly everything. *And*, at the same time, the transformation will be slower, messier, and more uneven than the headlines promise. The trillion-dollar figures are ceilings, not forecasts. The gains will lag. Ninety-five percent of corporate pilots are failing right now. The revolution is real, but it is not punctual.

Both of those are true at once. The breathless optimist and the weary skeptic are each clutching one half of the truth, and the task is to refuse to choose between them — to take the technology seriously *and* take the hype skeptically.

And let me say one thing against fatalism, because disruption is not destiny. The very same recent economy that gives us the broken bottom rung also did something hopeful: over the last two decades the share of humanity in extreme poverty fell from roughly thirty percent to about ten; and in the tight years after the pandemic, the lowest-paid workers won the biggest raises, reversing about a quarter of forty years' worth of widening inequality. Economies can deliver broad, surprising gains when the conditions are right. Which future we get from AI is, to a real degree, a choice — ours, not the machine's.

So let me end with three convictions I'd stake my own work on.

First: **learn the tools, personally.** Not because a chatbot will replace you, but because someone who has learned to work with these tools may well outperform someone who hasn't. That fifty-six percent wage premium is not a forecast — it's already here. The divide of the coming decade is not human versus machine. It's people-with-AI versus people-without.

Second: **protect the ladder, and double down on what the machine cannot do.** Anyone leading an organization should think hard before automating away every junior role to save money this year, because that is where the seniors of ten years from now are supposed to come from. And for each of us: the machine cannot take responsibility. It cannot be accountable. It cannot sit with a frightened person and earn their trust, or make the judgment call that someone's livelihood rests on and then own the consequences. As AI absorbs the routine, the distinctly human parts of our work don't shrink — they become the whole point. Empathy, judgment, the wisdom to know when the confident answer is wrong: these are appreciating assets in an age of cheap intelligence.

And third: **stay honest, including with yourself.** Remember the lawyer who trusted the machine. His failure wasn't that he used AI; it was that he trusted it without checking, and then trusted it again when it told him what he wanted to hear. The most dangerous property of these systems is that they are fluent and confident even when they are completely wrong. They are built to *sound* right. And sounding right and being right are very different things — the gap between them is exactly where human expertise still lives.

*[pause.]*

Seventy years ago, a group of mathematicians predicted machines would think within twenty years. They were wrong for a very long time. And then, almost overnight, they were right enough to change the world.

We are standing at the beginning of that change, not the end of it. We can't yet see which forecasts will hold — the optimists or the skeptics, the seven percent or the one. But we can choose how to meet it: with curiosity instead of fear, with judgment instead of credulity, and with a clear sense that the most valuable thing in a world full of artificial intelligence will, for a long time yet, remain the human one.

Thank you.
